Graph

<h1>Graph</h1>

Defination

Graph theory is the study of graphs, which are mathematical structures used to model relationships between objects.

Consistence of Graphs

A graph consists of:

1-Vertices (or nodes)

The points or objects in the graph.

2-Edges (or links)

The connections between the vertices.

Example

A social network where people are nodes and friendships are edges

Types of Graphs

1. Undirected graph: Edges have no direction (friendship is mutual).
2. Directed graph (digraph): Edges have a direction (like Twitter followers).
3. Weighted graph: Edges carry a value, e.g., distance, cost, or time.
4. Simple graph: No loops (edge from a vertex to itself) or multiple edges.
5. Complete graph: Every vertex is connected to every other vertex.

Key Concepts

• Degree of a vertex: Number of edges connected to it.
o In a digraph: in-degree and out-degree.
• Path: A sequence of vertices connected by edges.
• Cycle: A path that starts and ends at the same vertex.
• Connected graph: A graph where there’s a path between every pair of vertices.
• Tree: A connected graph with no cycles.
• Subgraph: A smaller graph made from some vertices and edges of the original graph.

Networks in Graph Theory

•Networks are graphs with additional information, often used to solve real-life problems.
• Transport networks: Cities as nodes, roads/railways as edges.
• Communication networks: Computers or phones as nodes, connections as edges.
• Flow networks: Water pipes, traffic, or data transfer; edges have capacities.

Key network concepts:

• Shortest path problem: Finding the minimum distance between nodes (Dijkstra’s algorithm).
• Minimum spanning tree: Connecting all vertices with the smallest total edge weight (Kruskal’s or Prim’s algorithm).
• Network flow: Maximum flow from a source to a sink (Ford-Fulkerson algorithm).

Applications

Graph theory and networks are used in:
• Internet routing and social networks
• GPS and mapping systems
• Project planning (PERT/CPM charts)
• Epidemiology (tracking disease spread)
• Electrical circuits and logistics

Simple Example

Imagine 4 cities (A, B, C, D) connected like this:
• A–B (5 km)
• A–C (10 km)
• B–C (2 km)
• B–D (4 km)
• C–D (1 km)
You can represent this as a weighted graph and use algorithms to:
• Find the shortest route from A to D
